Columbiad Launch Services is a small aerospace company based in Kitchener Ontario. Their focus is researching and developing a nano-thermite rocket fuel system for in-space propulsion. The proposed advantages of this type of fuel is combining the high energy density of solid fuels while allowing scalability and control similar to a liquid engine. While I was at Columbiad I worked on the design of a 3D printed combustion chamber, the design of a pressurant tank as a method of feeding the fuel into the combustion chamber, and conducted research into different ignition methods of the fuel.

3D Printed Combustion Chamber

My main focus was the design of the combustion chamber and integrated nozzle. This component was 3D printed out of an Inconel alloy, with help from MSAM at the University of Waterloo. The part was designed in SolidWorks and analysis was done with SolidWorks FEA and in Ansys. This analysis were done to ensure that the part could withstand the pressures and temperatures generated from combustion. Additionally, due to the nature of 3D printing, I had to ensure that the design would be geometrically possible to print.

Pressurant Tank Design

In order to feed the fuel into the combustion chamber, we explored using nitrogen as a pressurant to force flow from the nano-thermites. This was chosen as it allowed us to control the flow rate using variable pressure regulators. My role on this project was to determine the sizing of the pressurant tank. To do this I implemented the following equation in a MatLab script to determine the required mass of nitrogen as we changed desired parameters for the engine. $$m_{0} = \frac{P_{u}V_{Prop}}{RT_{0}}(\frac{\gamma }{1-P_{Pres}/P_{0}})$$ In addition to sizing the tank, I drew a P&ID for the entire system.

Ignition Research

An interesting problem we had to tackle with this novel fuel was how to effectively ignite it. Part of my role at Columbiad was to research the viability of different ignition methods. Some of the methods I explored were using traditional spark plugs, using plasma spark plugs, and using a laser. I found research that supported the energy produced by commercially available lasers would be sufficient to ignite the nano-thermites. This conclusion was validated when we conducted similar experiments with our fuel. With that research as a starting point, I started to design a way to integrate a laser into the system to allow it to work with our combustion chamber.
